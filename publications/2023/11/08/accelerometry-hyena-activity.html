Accelerometer-based predictions of behaviour elucidate factors affecting the daily activity patterns of spotted hyenas

Minasandra, P., Jensen, F. H., et al

08 Nov 2023

  • Publication
  • Full text
  • Code

In this paper, we find some pretty cool things about hyena activity patterns using a combination of cool analyses, machine learning, and accelerometer sensors.

The daily activity patterns of social animals may be influenced by who they interact with, who they are, what they were doing the previous day, and probably many other such factors.

Spotted hyenas are such social animals. They live in huge clans of several dozens of individuals. At any time however, most hyenas are alone, or in small subgroups of a handful of others.

The CCAS team deployed several sensors on 5 of these hyenas. For around 40 days, we knew where they were (GPS), what they were saying (mic), and potentially what they were doing (accelerometer)

An accelerometer measures acceleration in the 3 dimensions. I used a machine learning approach called a ‘random forest classifier’ to know the hyenas’ behaviours.

Once we knew what the hyenas were doing, we verified that our classifier made sense. Hyenas are nocturnal. That means they are usually only active at night. Our predictions also said the same thing, which was good.

We found that hyenas can synchronise their activity patterns with other hyenas. For this, they need to have spent time together. BUT all hyenas who hung out don’t have to synchronise!

We also found that each hyena has a sort of unique activity pattern, but overall, the patterns were still pretty similar.

Hyenas that were more active one day weren’t necessarily less active on future days. This means they probably don’t make up for being tired in this way.

Overall, we used a pretty cool way to approach activity patterns in social animals that live in the wild. This has been pretty challenging in the past, so hopefully other studies like this one can help understand this stuff better.
